Week 8: Computer-Controlled Machining

Week 8 introduced us to computer-controlled machining, focusing primarily on CNC routers for cutting and shaping larger pieces. This technology bridges the gap between small-scale digital fabrication (like laser cutting and 3D printing) and industrial manufacturing, allowing for the creation of furniture, structures, and large components from materials like wood, acrylic, and aluminum.

Key Learning Outcomes

  • Understanding CNC router capabilities, limitations, and safety considerations
  • Learning to design parts suitable for CNC machining
  • Creating toolpaths and G-code for CNC operations
  • Selecting appropriate cutting tools and speeds for different materials
  • Implementing fixturing and workholding strategies

Equipment Used

  • ShopBot CNC router for larger components
  • Fusion 360 for CAD design and CAM programming
  • Various endmills and cutting tools
  • Plywood, MDF, and acrylic sheet materials

Assignment Deliverables

For this assignment, I had to:

  1. Design a large-scale object that could be fabricated with a CNC machine
  2. Create appropriate toolpaths for machining the parts
  3. Cut the parts using the CNC router
  4. Assemble and document the resulting object

My Project: Modular Solar Equipment Stand

Given my background at Barefoot College, I designed a modular stand for solar equipment that can be quickly assembled in remote locations. The design consists of:

  • A base platform with adjustable feet for uneven terrain
  • Vertical supports with integrated cable management channels
  • A tilting top platform for optimal solar panel positioning
  • Components that interlock using a "press-fit" approach, requiring minimal fasteners

Design Considerations

Several factors influenced my design decisions:

  • Material efficiency: Designing the components to minimize waste from a standard sheet of plywood
  • Assembly complexity: Creating a design that could be assembled without specialized tools
  • Stability: Ensuring the structure would be stable when loaded with solar equipment
  • Transportability: Making components that could be flat-packed for easy transport to remote locations

The CNC Process

The CNC machining process involved several key steps:

  1. Preparing the digital design in CAD software, ensuring all dimensions and tolerances were appropriate
  2. Creating toolpaths in CAM software, selecting appropriate tools, feeds, and speeds
  3. Setting up the CNC machine, including material fixturing and tool changes
  4. Running the machining operations, monitoring progress and making adjustments as needed
  5. Finishing the cut parts (sanding edges, removing tabs, applying finishes)

Challenges and Solutions

One significant challenge was designing joints that would be both strong and easy to assemble. I experimented with different press-fit joint designs, testing small prototypes before committing to the full-scale fabrication.

Another challenge was optimizing the cutting paths to minimize machine time while maintaining quality. This required a careful balance of tool selection, cutting depth, and feed rates. After several iterations, I achieved a toolpath strategy that produced clean cuts while being time-efficient.

Reflection

Computer-controlled machining has opened up new possibilities for my work at Barefoot College. The ability to design and fabricate large-scale components locally could significantly reduce costs and improve customization of our solar installations. This technology would be particularly valuable for creating mounting systems, enclosures, and other structural components that need to be adapted to specific environments.

I'm excited about combining this machining knowledge with the electronics skills from previous weeks to create integrated systems that incorporate both structural and functional electronic elements.
